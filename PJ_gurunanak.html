Guru Nanak, the founder of Sikhism and the first Sikh Guru, was a mystic poet and spiritual leader from Punjab whose compositions form a cornerstone of the Guru Granth Sahib. Born in Rai Bhoi Di Talwandi, his poetry in Punjabi reflects universal themes of devotion, equality, and oneness with the divine. Traveling widely, he spread messages of compassion and unity, influencing millions. His hymns, known as shabads, remain central to Sikh worship and Punjabi literature.

Books and Publications

Early Life and Education

Guru Nanak was born on April 15, 1469, in Rai Bhoi Di Talwandi (now Nankana Sahib, Pakistan). Raised in a Hindu family, he showed spiritual inclinations early, studying Hindu and Islamic scriptures. His education included Punjabi, Persian, and Sanskrit, but his profound insights came from divine inspiration, shaping his role as a spiritual poet.

Spiritual Journey

Rejecting worldly life, Guru Nanak embarked on spiritual journeys (udasis) across South Asia and the Middle East, engaging with diverse faiths. Accompanied by Mardana, he composed shabads that challenged ritualism and caste, advocating equality and devotion. In 1521, he founded Kartarpur, establishing the Sikh community and passing his teachings to Guru Angad.

Personal Life

Guru Nanak married Sulakhani in 1487 and had two sons, Sri Chand and Lakhmi Das. Despite family life, he prioritized spiritual pursuits, traveling widely with companion Mardana. Settling in Kartarpur later, he lived simply, farming and teaching, embodying his principles of honest work and devotion.
